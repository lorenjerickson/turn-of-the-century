# Language Style: Gothic Horror + Steampunk at the Turn of the Century

## Purpose

Use this skill when authoring setting text, item descriptions, NPC biographies, location copy, lore entries, rumours, handouts, or narrative flavour for a role-playing world steeped in gothic horror and steampunk sensibilities. The desired register is late Victorian to Edwardian: ornate without becoming impenetrable, grave without becoming melodramatic, and period-conscious without lapsing into parody.

The aim is to produce prose that feels as though it might have been printed in a penny weekly, a private journal, a scientific circular, a church tract, a sensational broadside, or a gentleman's memoir circa 1890–1910.

## Core Directives

1. Write in a turn-of-the-century English register.
2. Blend gothic dread with industrial modernity.
3. Prefer atmosphere, unease, and implication over explicit gore.
4. Let setting carry emotion: architecture, weather, machinery, and lamplight should do narrative work.
5. Treat invention as wondrous, hazardous, and morally ambiguous.
6. Draw from broad period conventions, not the distinctive voice of any single author.

## Tone and Mood

The prevailing mood should be one of anxious wonder. The world is advancing, yet every engine, telegraph wire, laboratory lamp, and iron bridge seems to cast a longer shadow than it dispels.

Favour these tonal qualities:

- melancholy
- dread
- solemn curiosity
- moral unease
- sepulchral grandeur
- scientific ambition touched by hubris
- social restraint covering private terror
- the sense that old evils persist beneath modern progress

Good emotional tensions include:

- reason versus superstition
- invention versus conscience
- civilisation versus decay
- class decorum versus private corruption
- imperial confidence versus spiritual rot
- public modernity versus ancestral guilt

## Essential Genre Ingredients

### Gothic Horror

Lean on classic gothic devices:

- decaying manors, crypts, bell towers, asylums, abbeys, tenements, laboratories, and fog-drowned streets
- oppressive interiors, concealed passages, locked rooms, old portraits, family papers, blackened mirrors, and dust-laden drapery
- the return of the past through bloodlines, curses, scandal, debt, obsession, or buried crime
- obscurity, silence, echoes, moonlight, candlelight, gaslight, and nocturnal weather
- psychological disturbance expressed through setting, architecture, and sensory detail
- journals, memoranda, sworn statements, newspaper clippings, and recovered correspondence as framing devices

### Steampunk

Use steampunk not as decoration alone, but as a material vocabulary of the setting:

- brass, copper, iron, rivets, pistons, pressure valves, flywheels, boilers, gauges, dynamos, galvanic coils, clockwork, pneumatic tubes, telegraphic apparatus, analytical engines, airships, automata, and surgical contrivances
- machines that are elegant, hand-wrought, and dangerous
- invention presented as the future imagined by the past
- craftsmanship and mechanism balanced with soot, noise, heat, and the threat of catastrophic failure
- scientific and engineering culture with societies, lectures, patents, workshops, observatories, and private laboratories

The strongest results arise when the machine heightens the horror: an automaton with too human a gesture, a respirator hissing in a mortuary, a brass lift descending to catacombs, a laboratory whose lamps never wholly banish the dark.

## Historical Language Guidance

Write in language that sounds natural for educated prose of the period. This does **not** mean using faux-medieval speech.

### Prefer

- formal, measured syntax
- longer sentences balanced by precise punctuation
- elevated but intelligible vocabulary
- restrained emotion that occasionally breaks into alarm
- period-appropriate intensifiers such as `most`, `singularly`, `exceedingly`, `rather`, `grave`, `peculiar`, `ghastly`, `infernal`, `lamentable`, `odious`, `extraordinary`
- courteous, class-conscious phrasing in dialogue and exposition

### Avoid

- modern slang and idiom
- casual contemporary rhythm
- internet-era understatement or irony
- therapy-speak, corporate-speak, and cinematic trailer language
- empty decorative adjectives piled without purpose
- gratuitous archaic forms like `thou`, `thee`, `forsooth`, or `mayhap` unless quoting a liturgy, antique text, or deliberately affected speaker

### Preferred Vocabulary Field

Use words and phrases of this general character:

- `most curious`
- `singular`
- `ghastly`
- `dreadful`
- `morbid`
- `melancholy`
- `sepulchral`
- `obscure`
- `unnatural`
- `lamentable`
- `infernal machine`
- `galvanic apparatus`
- `aetheric engine`
- `the small hours`
- `of a piece with`
- `it is said`
- `I daresay`
- `Good God`
- `I confess`
- `one cannot but`
- `it would appear`
- `under cover of fog`
- `beneath a lowering sky`

### Vocabulary to Avoid

Avoid words like these unless a speaker is deliberately anachronistic:

- `okay`
- `awesome`
- `creepy`
- `weird` (prefer `singular`, `peculiar`, `unnatural`, `uncanny`)
- `vibes`
- `high-tech`
- `sci-fi`
- `dark fantasy`
- `spooky`
- `super intense`
- `massive reveal`

## Sentence Style

The prose should feel period-authentic in cadence:

- Use sentences that unfold through observation, qualification, and ominous implication.
- Alternate dense descriptive sentences with short, grave conclusions.
- Permit rhetorical weight, but preserve clarity.
- Let descriptions accumulate dread by particulars rather than by shouting emotion.

Example rhythm:

- first, an exact observation
- next, a disquieting implication
- finally, a restrained but chilling judgment

## Imagery Priorities

When in doubt, privilege sensory details from these families:

- fog, soot, cinders, ash, rain, frost, iron damp, coal smoke
- gaslight halos, candle gutters, moonlit stone, green laboratory fire, crimson furnace-glow
- ticking escapements, steam-hiss, valve-rattle, distant bells, muffled wheels, telegraph chatter
- velvet gone threadbare, black lacquer, tarnished brass, cracked leather, old lace, mourning crepe
- mausolea, viaducts, conservatories, fever wards, pumping stations, rail depots, séance parlours, anatomical theatres

Architecture should often mirror the moral or mental condition of the place. A house may stoop. A corridor may seem to listen. A workshop may breathe like an ailing beast.

## Character Voice Guidance

### Narration

Narration should sound literate, observant, and faintly haunted. Even plain description ought to carry an undertone of apprehension.

### Scholars, Physicians, Detectives, Inventors

Give them disciplined diction, technical confidence, and occasional cracks in composure. They should attempt explanation before surrendering to dread.

### Aristocrats, Clergy, Widows, Antiquarians

Let them speak with formality, restraint, inherited certainty, and veiled scandal.

### Labourers, Cabmen, Constables, Servants

Reduce the register somewhat, but do not collapse into cartoon dialect. A few period turns are preferable to phonetic spelling.

## Horror Technique

Use the following principles when scenes should feel frightening:

- conceal before you reveal
- imply before you name
- let dread arrive through setting, not merely event
- use the past as an active pressure upon the present
- keep some uncertainty whenever possible
- present the monstrous as an affront to order, body, lineage, faith, or reason

Prefer terror, apprehension, and uncanny discovery over dismemberment and shock-description. The reader should feel that something is not merely dangerous, but profoundly out of joint with the natural and moral order.

## Steampunk Technique

When describing technology:

- make it tactile and mechanical
- name parts, materials, motions, and consequences
- emphasise ingenuity, labour, heat, pressure, and maintenance
- allow beauty and menace to coexist
- remember that invention changes class, labour, medicine, transport, surveillance, and war

The machine should never feel like plastic futurism. It should feel built by hands, powered by fuel, housed in wood and metal, and prone to smoke, vibration, and failure.

## Fin-de-Siecle Concerns

To reinforce the turn-of-the-century atmosphere, allow the world to brood upon concerns common to the age:

- degeneration and decline beneath apparent progress
- industrial blight and urban crowding
- spiritualism, séances, mesmerism, and occult societies
- medical experimentation and dubious scientific ethics
- inherited vice, family disgrace, and public scandal
- anxieties over empire, class unrest, disease, vice, and the unseen life of the city

## Practical Writing Rules

- Keep prose evocative, but useful for play.
- Give concrete hooks: names, rumours, symptoms, odours, stains, devices, habits, and social consequences.
- Avoid generic fantasy phrasing if a period-specific alternative exists.
- Use proper nouns sparingly but memorably.
- If writing item or location text, combine utility with flavour.
- If writing lore, make it sound archived, whispered, redacted, sworn, or half-forgotten.

## Authoring Patterns

### Location Description Pattern

1. Establish the place in material terms.
2. Introduce the atmospheric disturbance.
3. Hint at buried history, scandal, or experiment.
4. End with a line that suggests danger or fascination.

### NPC Description Pattern

1. Give carriage, dress, and class markers.
2. Add one unsettling trait.
3. Suggest a private motive, sorrow, obsession, or disgrace.
4. Let speech reveal education and temperament.

### Item Description Pattern

1. Name material and workmanship.
2. Explain practical function.
3. Add a whisper of reputation, malfunction, stain, provenance, or moral cost.

## Mini Examples

### Example A: Modern Flat

`A creepy machine in the basement gives off weird vibes.`

### Example A: Preferred

`In the cellar there stands a singular apparatus of brass and blackened iron, whose valves exhale at intervals a warm and fetid vapour. No servant will remain alone with it after midnight, and the engineer who devised it has not been seen these three weeks.`

### Example B: Modern Flat

`The city is full of danger and corruption.`

### Example B: Preferred

`By day the city proclaims itself a monument to progress; by night it reveals the price of that boast in soot-clogged alleys, shuttered infirmaries, and drawing rooms where respectable persons speak in whispers of things no police report will name.`

## Final Constraint

The prose should feel haunted by the age itself: devout yet doubtful, industrious yet decaying, civilised yet cruel, convinced of progress yet forever glancing over its shoulder into the fog.

# Science, Not Magic: Artifacts and Abilities in a Gothic-Steampunk World

## Purpose

Use this skill when generating artifacts, devices, procedures, formulas, disciplines, abilities, upgrades, talents, techniques, ordeals, and extraordinary effects for a role-playing world in which science, engineering, medicine, electricity, chemistry, mesmerism, and obscure research occupy the narrative space that fantasy settings often reserve for magic.

The governing principle is simple: effects may appear uncanny, arcane, or otherworldly, yet they must always be framed as phenomena with material causes, observable symptoms, experimental limits, and practical consequences. The world may not fully understand every mechanism, but it should still treat such matters as something to be studied, tested, measured, patented, feared, or suppressed.

In short: the strange is permissible; the supernatural vocabulary of fantasy is not.

## Core Directives

1. Present extraordinary effects as scientific, technical, physiological, or empirical phenomena.
2. Avoid fantasy-magic terminology in mechanics, flavour, and naming.
3. Describe advanced capabilities as disciplined applications of apparatus, training, chemistry, electricity, pressure, vibration, optics, nerves, memory, or other material principles.
4. Let observers mistake advanced science for sorcery, but never let the setting itself rely upon sorcerous assumptions.
5. Preserve mystery while maintaining an undercurrent of measurement, repeatability, and consequence.
6. Draw on broad late Victorian and Edwardian scientific imagination rather than modern techno-jargon or fantasy spellcraft.

## Conceptual Frame

This world follows the old insight that sufficiently advanced science may be mistaken for magic. That confusion is useful for tone, but it must remain a confusion of perception, not a statement of truth.

An effect may be:

- poorly understood
- dangerous to reproduce
- dependent on rare materials
- unstable under field conditions
- known only to a secret society or laboratory
- recorded only in fragmentary journals
- spoken of in whispers by laymen as if it were witchcraft

Yet it should still imply:

- a mechanism, however incomplete
- a chain of cause and effect
- sensory evidence
- measurable conditions
- observed failures
- side effects or operational costs

## Tone and Intellectual Style

The tone should be grave, exacting, and faintly horrified by its own discoveries. Researchers, physicians, engineers, and occult natural philosophers may disagree profoundly, yet each should speak as though evidence matters.

Favour these tonal qualities:

- measured curiosity
- institutional secrecy
- experimental ambition
- moral uncertainty
- clinical dread
- patent-era optimism undercut by disaster
- the sense that human knowledge has advanced faster than human wisdom

## Sources of the Extraordinary

When inventing abilities or artifacts, root them in one or more plausible-seeming domains:

- galvanism and electrical discharge
- chemistry, reagents, vapours, salts, toxins, and accelerants
- surgery, anatomy, nerves, reflexes, endocrine or humoral theories
- optics, lenses, phosphors, mirrors, polarisation, lantern projection
- acoustics, resonance, vibration, infrasonic or ultrasonic effects in period language
- magnetism, induction, static charge, dynamos, coils, Leyden jars
- pressure systems, pneumatics, steam, vacuum chambers, compressed gases
- bacteriology, pathology, contagion, inoculation, degeneration, hereditary taint
- hypnotism, mesmerism, suggestion, conditioned response, memory disturbance
- rare minerals, meteorites, radium-like substances, luminous compounds, occulted elements
- analytical engines, punched logic, automata, clockwork computation, coded telegraphy

The explanation need not be scientifically modern. It must merely be empirical in posture: somebody observed it, catalogued it, replicated it imperfectly, and paid for the knowledge.

## Naming Conventions

Names should sound like instruments, patents, clinical syndromes, military prototypes, society jargon, or newspaper shorthand rather than fantasy incantations.

### Good Naming Patterns

- `The Havelock Induction Harness`
- `Noctilucent Reagent No. 4`
- `Somnolent Pressure Apparatus`
- `Galvanic Revivifier`
- `Aetheric Relay Lens`
- `Vitreous Resonance Plate`
- `Mourning Gas, Refined`
- `The Brier Method`
- `Instrument for Cerebral Excitation`
- `Pneumatic Surgical Hand`
- `Electro-Somnambulist Discipline`
- `The Blackwell Conduction Coil`

### Weak Naming Patterns

- `Shadow Spell`
- `Soul Drain`
- `Arcane Bolt`
- `Necromancer's Grasp`
- `Mage Armour`

If a common nickname becomes lurid or superstitious, keep an official technical name nearby.

Example:

- street name: `the Widow's Lantern`
- formal name: `Portable Phosphor-Induction Survey Lamp`

## Describing Abilities

Abilities should read as trained procedures, physiological adaptations, experimental treatments, or technical deployments.

Each ability should imply:

1. a method
2. a material basis
3. a limit
4. a cost or risk
5. an observable effect

### Good Ability Logic

- A subject has undergone nerve conditioning and may briefly ignore pain under controlled stimulation.
- A field operator carries a tuned lens assembly that reveals traces of certain vapours or residues invisible to the naked eye.
- An investigator uses an acoustic resonator to dislodge concealed latches, disturb vermin in walls, or provoke hallucinations in susceptible listeners.

### Poor Ability Logic

- A character channels mystic force to do whatever the scene requires.
- An effect occurs with no apparatus, training, pathology, or observable mechanism.

## Describing Artifacts

Artifacts should feel built, assembled, modified, repaired, tested, and feared. They may be beautiful, but they should never feel like abstract magical tokens.

Always consider:

- materials
- visible components
- operating method
- maintenance burden
- failure state
- provenance
- scandal attached to invention or use

### Preferred Artifact Anatomy

An artifact often includes:

- a housing of brass, iron, wood, leather, gutta-percha, glass, or vulcanite
- visible screws, valves, coils, needles, reservoirs, mirrors, wires, seals, straps, or cartridges
- heat, odour, vibration, residue, discharge marks, stains, or audible signs of operation

## Limits and Costs

The setting becomes more convincing when extraordinary science is constrained.

Use costs such as:

- rare reagents
- battery drain or pressure loss
- overheating
- contamination
- tremor, nausea, insomnia, nosebleeds, neural fatigue, memory damage, tissue burns
- social stigma or legal prohibition
- danger to operator, patient, bystander, or structure
- the need for calibration, darkness, silence, blood sample, specific frequency, specific temperature, or controlled dosage

An ability without cost feels magical. An ability with a procedure, side effect, and failure mode feels scientific.

## Mystery Without Mysticism

To keep the world uncanny without lapsing into fantasy, use phrases that imply incomplete but serious investigation.

### Prefer Language Like

- `no fully satisfactory explanation has yet been produced`
- `the effect is consistent, though not well understood`
- `the subject exhibits repeatable symptoms`
- `observed under laboratory conditions`
- `field results vary considerably`
- `the apparatus performs beyond current theory`
- `the physicians disagree as to cause`
- `its action appears to depend upon atmospheric conditions`
- `the residue suggests a catalytic process`
- `a phenomenon of nervous transmission`

### Avoid Language Like

- `by sorcery`
- `through raw magic`
- `enchanted by unknown means`
- `the gods grant this power`

If common folk use such phrases in dialogue, frame them as frightened misunderstanding.

## Scientific-Flavoured Domains of Effect

Below are exemplary categories of extraordinary effects and how to frame them.

### Offensive Effects

Frame as:

- electrical discharge
- thermal burst
- corrosive spray
- pressurised dart or gas release
- focused acoustic or optical disruption
- nervous shock or induced seizure

### Defensive Effects

Frame as:

- insulated clothing
- pressure armour
- chemical hardening
- deflection screens of wire mesh, mirrored plates, or charged fields
- physiological analgesia
- smoke, glare, acoustic baffling, or interference

### Perception Effects

Frame as:

- enhanced optics
- residue detection
- auditory amplification
- pattern analysis
- pathological sensitivity
- conditioned intuition built from observation and training

### Mobility Effects

Frame as:

- spring-loaded mechanisms
- pneumatic braces
- climbing gauntlets
- traction compounds
- stimulants that increase exertion for brief periods at bodily cost

### Restoration Effects

Frame as:

- emergency surgery
- transfusion-like procedures
- galvanic revival
- stimulant compounds
- nerve tonics
- antiseptic or regenerative dressings

Restoration should feel imperfect, costly, and alarming rather than cleanly miraculous.

## The Uncanny Body

In this world, the body may be altered, corrected, disciplined, revived, conditioned, or ruined by science.

Use bodily language that is anatomical, clinical, and material:

- pulse
- tissue
- nerve
- reflex
- lesion
- secretion
- tremor
- respiration
- dilation
- degeneration
- adaptation
- excitability
- muscular response

This supports an otherworldly feel without invoking fantasy metaphysics.

## Social and Institutional Context

Abilities and artifacts should belong to systems of power.

Ask:

- who funds this research?
- who patents or suppresses it?
- which hospital, regiment, ministry, guild, salon, or clandestine society controls access?
- who is used as a test subject?
- what scandal, rumour, or public panic surrounds it?

Scientific wonder in this setting should often carry a political, class, or ethical shadow.

## Authoring Patterns

### Artifact Pattern

1. Name the device in technical or patent style.
2. Describe material construction.
3. Explain operating principle in plausible period terms.
4. State practical effect.
5. Add side effect, failure case, or scandal.

### Ability Pattern

1. Identify training, treatment, or apparatus required.
2. State the observable phenomenon.
3. Describe what bystanders perceive.
4. Clarify operational limit.
5. End with physiological or technical cost.

### Procedure Pattern

1. Specify tools, compounds, and conditions.
2. Note duration and required precision.
3. Explain expected outcome.
4. Mention dangerous deviations.

## Mini Examples

### Example A: Fantasy Flat

`The doctor casts a healing spell to close the wound.`

### Example A: Preferred

`The doctor applies the Brier galvanic protocol, binding copper contacts above the wound and administering a stimulant saline whose action forces a rapid, painful contraction of the damaged tissue. The bleeding slows at once, though the patient will suffer tremor and fever before morning.`

### Example B: Fantasy Flat

`The investigator uses a divination spell to see hidden things.`

### Example B: Preferred

`The investigator fits a smoked-lens detection frame over one eye and exposes the room to a measured pulse from a phosphor induction lamp. Certain residues, invisible in ordinary light, answer with a pale and oily fluorescence along the skirting boards and about the locked escritoire.`

### Example C: Fantasy Flat

`The soldier enchants his gauntlet and fires a magic bolt.`

### Example C: Preferred

`The gauntlet's condenser is wound to its final tolerance and discharged through a forked brass emitter, producing a brief blue arc that seizes the target's musculature and leaves the air sharp with ozone. A second discharge without cooling would very likely split the casing.`

## Final Constraint

Every artifact and ability should feel as though it emerged from workshops, laboratories, infirmaries, observatories, and forbidden lecture halls rather than from wizardry. However strange the result, the prose must suggest experiment, observation, and consequence. The proper response of the world is not `What spell is this?` but `What in God's name have they built, and how often has it been tested?`

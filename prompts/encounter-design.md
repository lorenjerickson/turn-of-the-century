# Encounter Generation Prep Prompt

You are designing an encounter for Turn of the Century, a gothic-horror and steampunk roleplaying game set around the 1890s. An encounter is a focused moment where player heroes come into meaningful contact with pawns, villains, hazards, evidence, machinery, social pressure, or hostile circumstances.

The encounter must feel period-appropriate: late Victorian or Edwardian in language, social concerns, technology, atmosphere, and moral unease. Blend gothic dread with industrial modernity. Prefer fog, soot, gaslight, rail platforms, laboratories, hospitals, asylums, séance parlours, factories, tenements, observatories, estates, newspaper offices, telegraph rooms, warehouses, pumping stations, railway cuttings, and lonely roads over generic fantasy spaces.

Extraordinary events must be framed through advanced science, engineering, medicine, psychology, chemistry, electricity, heredity, mesmerism, rare materials, experimental apparatus, or poorly understood natural phenomena. Do not rely on fantasy magic as an explanation. Characters may mistake strange science for sorcery, but the encounter itself should imply material causes, observable effects, limits, costs, and consequences.

## Encounter Requirements

Create an encounter with:

1. Encounter purpose:
   - why this encounter exists in the scenario or campaign
   - whether it is planned as part of the story arc or generated randomly during travel
   - what information, pressure, danger, choice, or consequence it introduces
2. Current location context:
   - the exact location or type of location where the party is now
   - how the encounter environment reflects that location
   - whether the encounter references an existing location or creates a new one
   - whether an associated map is useful, required, or unnecessary
3. Environment:
   - terrain, architecture, light, weather, sound, odours, machinery, visibility, cover, thresholds, exits, crowds, animals, vehicles, or unstable structures
   - hazards that matter mechanically or narratively
   - details the GM can use immediately at the table
   - how the environment changes if the encounter escalates
4. Participants:
   - player heroes are assumed to be present
   - include or create all required pawns and villains
   - identify each pawn or villain by role, attitude, motive, and immediate behaviour
   - not all pawns should be hostile by default; include neutral, frightened, misled, desperate, protective, bribed, or persuadable figures when appropriate
   - reserve villains for major threats, leaders, bosses, or recurring adversaries
5. Hidden or delayed actors:
   - include actors not visible at the start, such as hidden enemies, watchers, trapped victims, informants, ambushers, or concealed operators
   - include waves of reinforcements when appropriate
   - describe when and why each hidden or delayed actor appears
   - explain how perceptive or cautious heroes might discover them early
6. Success conditions:
   - what counts as success
   - whether success means protecting a pawn, recovering an artifact, escaping, surviving, gathering evidence, exposing a fraud, calming a crowd, repairing apparatus, winning cooperation, or defeating a threat
   - what counts as partial success
   - what proof or consequence confirms the outcome
7. Failure conditions:
   - what happens if the heroes fail, flee, delay, or misjudge the situation
   - what becomes worse but still playable
   - which NPCs, clues, artifacts, locations, or relationships are lost, damaged, or transformed
8. Encounter type:
   - identify whether the encounter is primarily social, investigative, exploratory, hazardous, chase-oriented, technical, survival-focused, combat-capable, or combat-forward
   - do not force combat when negotiation, stealth, evidence, medicine, engineering, or restraint would be more interesting
   - if combat is likely, explain what might prevent it or end it early
9. Player choices:
   - at least three meaningful approaches the heroes might take
   - how pawns and villains react to those approaches
   - how the environment rewards observation, preparation, and clever use
10. Scenario or travel integration:
   - for planned encounters, explain how this encounter advances the scenario goal
   - for random travel encounters, explain why it belongs in the current region, road, neighbourhood, rail line, river, sea lane, or wilderness
   - identify any clues, rumours, injuries, debts, or consequences that can carry forward

## Style Requirements

Write in a grave, literate, turn-of-the-century register. Use period-conscious prose without parody. Avoid modern slang, cinematic trailer language, and generic fantasy phrasing.

The encounter should evoke anxious wonder: progress casting a longer shadow than it dispels. Let dread emerge through implication, architecture, weather, machinery, institutional secrecy, family disgrace, public scandal, medical risk, dangerous invention, and scientific hubris.

## Output Priorities

The generated encounter should help a GM place heroes, pawns, villains, hazards, and objectives into immediate contact. It should clearly identify or create required locations and actors, support noncombat as well as combat play, account for hidden or delayed participants, and provide success and failure conditions that matter beyond the encounter.

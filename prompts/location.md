# Location Generation Prep Prompt

You are designing a location for Turn of the Century, a gothic-horror and steampunk roleplaying game set around the 1890s. Locations may vary greatly in scale: a continent, region, city, town, village, district, market, forest, desert campsite, mountain pass, railway station, estate, hospital, dockside, or other place that can anchor travel, scenarios, encounters, and maps.

The location must feel period-appropriate: late Victorian or Edwardian in language, social concerns, technology, atmosphere, and moral unease. Blend gothic dread with industrial modernity. Prefer fog, soot, gaslight, rail lines, telegraph wires, laboratories, hospitals, asylums, factories, tenements, observatories, estates, market squares, ports, roads, camps, and weathered frontiers over generic fantasy spaces.

Extraordinary details must be framed through advanced science, engineering, medicine, psychology, chemistry, electricity, heredity, mesmerism, rare materials, experimental apparatus, or poorly understood natural phenomena. Do not rely on fantasy magic as an explanation. Local superstition may misread strange science, disease, machinery, or natural phenomena, but the location itself should imply material causes, observable signs, limits, costs, and consequences.

## Location Requirements

Create a location with:

1. Scale and type:
   - identify whether it is a world, continent, region, city, town, village, district, borough, market, campsite, wilderness site, building complex, or other place
   - choose the most appropriate location type for the system
   - make the description suit the scale; North America, a Romanian forest, Athens, a fishing village, and a desert campsite require different levels of detail
2. Map position and parent geography:
   - describe the location's general position on the world map
   - identify or propose the parent location it belongs under
   - explain how the parent location shapes climate, travel, culture, architecture, risk, and available resources
   - make a campsite in the desert feel fundamentally different from a campsite in the Alps, a London market, or a Black Sea village
3. Environmental description:
   - terrain, weather, light, sound, odour, flora, fauna, architecture, roads, rail lines, waterways, machinery, public spaces, private thresholds, and signs of class or institutional power
   - what a party notices immediately on arrival
   - what changes by season, hour, weather, crowding, or danger
   - whether the location should have an associated map, and what that map should emphasise
4. Obvious details:
   - landmarks, entrances, exits, hazards, common inhabitants, sounds, smells, local industries, patrols, transport, and public rumours
   - details the GM can state openly without requiring investigation
5. Discoverable details:
   - concealed rooms, private routes, coded signs, local secrets, buried evidence, unreliable witnesses, hidden machinery, medical traces, old scandals, unusual tracks, strange residues, or suppressed records
   - what skills, social access, professions, tools, or observations might reveal them
   - how discovery changes the party's understanding of the place
6. Features:
   - several named features that can be referenced in scenarios or encounters
   - each feature should have a clear purpose, atmosphere, and possible use at the table
   - include at least one feature suitable for social play, one for investigation, and one for danger or tension when the scale permits
7. Local context:
   - dominant social tensions, institutions, factions, trades, families, officials, or informal powers
   - what outsiders misunderstand about the place
   - what locals refuse to say plainly
   - what has recently changed or begun to go wrong
8. Scenario and encounter utility:
   - why heroes might come here
   - what kinds of scenarios can begin, turn, or end here
   - what kinds of encounters naturally fit this location
   - what pawns or villains are likely to be found here or connected to it
9. Travel and access:
   - how the party reaches the location
   - what makes arrival easy, costly, dangerous, suspicious, or seasonal
   - what routes, checkpoints, terrain, weather, or social barriers affect movement
10. Tone and secrets:
   - the emotional impression the location leaves
   - the ordinary face it presents to the world
   - the troubling implication beneath that surface

## Style Requirements

Write in a grave, literate, turn-of-the-century register. Use period-conscious prose without parody. Avoid modern slang, cinematic trailer language, and generic fantasy phrasing.

The location should evoke anxious wonder: progress casting a longer shadow than it dispels. Let dread emerge through implication, architecture, weather, machinery, institutional secrecy, family disgrace, public scandal, medical risk, dangerous invention, geography, and inherited memory.

## Output Priorities

The generated location should help a GM place the site on the world map, understand its parent geography, describe what is obvious to the party, preserve meaningful discoveries for play, and reuse the location in scenarios, encounters, travel, and maps.

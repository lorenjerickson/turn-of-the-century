# Actor Generation Prep Prompt

You are designing an actor for Turn of the Century, a gothic-horror and steampunk roleplaying game set around the 1890s. Actors may be heroes, pawns, or villains. The GM will specify the actor type before generation.

The actor must feel period-appropriate: late Victorian or Edwardian in name, profession, social position, equipment, anxieties, habits, and language. Blend gothic dread with industrial modernity. Prefer physicians, constables, inventors, clerks, porters, spiritualists, labourers, aristocrats, journalists, antiquarians, soldiers, inspectors, servants, scholars, factory hands, and society figures over generic fantasy roles.

Extraordinary capabilities must be framed through advanced science, training, medicine, psychology, chemistry, electricity, heredity, mesmerism, rare materials, experimental apparatus, disciplined practice, or poorly understood natural phenomena. Do not rely on fantasy magic as an explanation. A character may be rumoured to be uncanny, cursed, or monstrous, but the underlying description should imply material causes, observable signs, limits, costs, and consequences.

## Actor Type Requirements

Create the requested actor type:

1. Hero:
   - a player-facing protagonist or important allied figure
   - include an archetype, rank, bonds, profession, personal drive, useful equipment, quirks, and talents
   - make them capable but unfinished, with hooks that invite player ownership or GM adaptation
2. Pawn:
   - a supporting NPC, creature, bystander, witness, servant, thug, specialist, victim, informant, investigator, guard, or lesser threat
   - include role, threat, disposition, faction, motives, equipment, quirks, talents, and what they know or conceal
   - pawns need not be hostile; they may be frightened, neutral, bribed, loyal, misled, desperate, protective, or persuadable
3. Villain:
   - a major antagonist, boss, mastermind, recurring threat, or dangerous patron
   - include scheme, threat tier, notoriety, lieutenants or followers, public face, private vice, equipment, quirks, talents, and methods of pressure
   - make the villain's goal intelligible even when morally abhorrent

## Actor Requirements

Create an actor with:

1. Name and identity:
   - period-appropriate full name
   - public role, profession, class position, faction, and social mask
   - private fear, ambition, shame, loyalty, or obsession
2. Table usefulness:
   - immediate reason the GM might introduce this actor
   - what the actor wants right now
   - what the actor can offer, obstruct, reveal, or endanger
   - how the actor might recur in later scenarios
3. Abilities and competencies:
   - ability scores and skills that match the concept
   - one or two areas of real competence
   - one weakness, blind spot, or exploitable pressure
4. Equipment:
   - practical period-appropriate possessions, tools, clothing, weapons, armour, documents, instruments, or apparatus
   - equipment should feel carried, maintained, worn, repaired, or improvised
   - include suggested equipment names even when exact item ids are unavailable
5. Quirks and talents:
   - quirks should be memorable behavioural, social, physiological, professional, or moral details
   - talents should feel like training, discipline, social leverage, technical skill, fieldcraft, medical practice, or experimental technique
   - avoid fantasy spell-like powers
6. Social and narrative context:
   - relationship to one or more locations, factions, scenarios, encounters, or campaign tensions
   - who trusts them, fears them, pays them, protects them, or wants them silenced
7. Presentation:
   - a concise visual impression
   - manner of speech
   - tells, habits, tools, scent, dress, injuries, papers, or sounds that make the actor distinct
8. Secrets and discoveries:
   - what is obvious to the heroes
   - what requires conversation, investigation, leverage, or observation
   - what the actor will do under pressure

## Style Requirements

Write in a grave, literate, turn-of-the-century register. Use period-conscious prose without parody. Avoid modern slang, cinematic trailer language, and generic fantasy phrasing.

The actor should evoke anxious wonder: progress casting a longer shadow than it dispels. Let dread emerge through implication, class position, illness, reputation, machinery, institutional secrecy, family disgrace, public scandal, medical risk, dangerous invention, and moral compromise.

## Output Priorities

The generated actor should be immediately usable in play, with a clear type, strong identity, useful table hooks, plausible equipment, quirks, talents, and enough mechanical shape to create a Foundry actor of the selected type.

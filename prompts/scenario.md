# Scenario Generation Prep Prompt

You are designing a scenario for Turn of the Century, a gothic-horror and steampunk roleplaying game set around the 1890s. A scenario is a compact campaign arc intended to run to completion in six or fewer game sessions.

The scenario must feel period-appropriate: late Victorian or Edwardian in language, social concerns, technology, atmosphere, and moral unease. Blend gothic dread with industrial modernity. Prefer fog, soot, gaslight, rail stations, laboratories, hospitals, asylums, séance parlours, factories, tenements, observatories, estates, newspapers, telegraph offices, and learned societies over generic fantasy locations.

Extraordinary events must be framed through advanced science, engineering, medicine, psychology, chemistry, electricity, heredity, mesmerism, rare materials, experimental apparatus, or poorly understood natural phenomena. Do not rely on fantasy magic as an explanation. Characters may mistake strange science for sorcery, but the scenario itself should imply material causes, observable effects, limits, costs, and consequences.

## Scenario Requirements

Create a scenario with:

1. A clear scenario goal:
   - what the player characters are trying to accomplish
   - why it matters now
   - how the goal connects to a larger campaign, if applicable
   - what hidden truth or campaign element may be discovered
2. Success conditions:
   - what counts as full success
   - what counts as partial success
   - what evidence, person, device, location, or social outcome confirms success
3. Failure conditions:
   - what happens if the characters delay, choose poorly, or are defeated
   - what becomes worse but still playable
   - what facts remain discoverable after failure
   - how failure complicates future scenarios rather than ending the story
4. Location or locations:
   - primary location where the scenario is centred
   - secondary locations that may be visited
   - whether any location should have an associated map
   - what each location contributes to investigation, conflict, atmosphere, or choice
5. Detailed environment:
   - architecture, weather, light, sounds, odours, machinery, furnishings, and signs of class or institutional power
   - how the environment changes as the scenario escalates
   - hazards, obstacles, hiding places, thresholds, locked rooms, machinery, crowds, or difficult terrain
   - details the GM can use immediately at the table
6. Significant NPCs:
   - likely pawns, witnesses, victims, experts, servants, rivals, officials, suspects, patrons, or compromised allies
   - what each NPC wants
   - what each NPC knows
   - what each NPC will conceal, distort, or misunderstand
   - how NPCs may become recurring campaign figures
7. Antagonistic pressure:
   - include a clear antagonist only if the scenario requires one
   - otherwise identify the opposing force, such as disease, institutional secrecy, dangerous machinery, public panic, social scandal, environmental collapse, or a hidden experiment
   - describe how this pressure acts if the players do nothing
8. Encounters:
   - identify several encounter concepts the scenario will likely contain
   - each encounter should have a purpose, location, involved NPCs or hazards, and likely stakes
   - encounters should support investigation, social manoeuvring, exploration, danger, and consequential choice
   - do not fully design encounter mechanics here; provide enough context for later encounter generation
9. Multiple paths to success:
   - at least three distinct approaches, such as investigation, negotiation, infiltration, technical repair, medical intervention, public exposure, legal pressure, pursuit, or direct confrontation
   - meaningful consequences for each approach
   - clues or opportunities that prevent the scenario from depending on a single die roll or single scene
10. Session pacing:
   - a suggested breakdown for one to six sessions
   - opening pressure, midpoint complication, escalation, and resolution
   - optional scenes that can be omitted if the table needs a shorter scenario

## Style Requirements

Write in a grave, literate, turn-of-the-century register. Use period-conscious prose without parody. Avoid modern slang, cinematic trailer language, and generic fantasy phrasing.

The scenario should evoke anxious wonder: progress casting a longer shadow than it dispels. Let dread emerge through implication, architecture, weather, machinery, institutional secrecy, family disgrace, public scandal, medical risk, and scientific hubris.

## Output Priorities

The generated scenario should help a GM run a short arc with clear stakes, vivid locations, important NPCs, several viable routes to success, and consequences that can feed a larger campaign. It should be immediately useful as a scenario seed, with enough structure to generate encounters, pawns, and locations later.
